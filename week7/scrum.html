Scrum Process

Scrum is a popular form of Agile. It follows the values listed in the Agile Manifesto as well as the 12 Principles of Agile. Scrum is a methodology for project management. Other forms of Agile, such as XP (extreme programming) and Kanban focus on different aspects of software development. For example, although it also follows the values in the manifesto and the 12 principles, XP focuses more on improving the process of writing code than Scrum, which focuses on improving team communication and interaction.

In Scrum terms, developing software means implementing features specified in the backlog. See here for a definition of backlog. The team decides which features to work on, often a product owner is involved. See here for a description of the role of the product owner.

Scrum development is done in cycles. One iteration of the cycle is a sprint. A sprint has four events:

  1. The sprint planning session, where the team decides what part of the backlog to develop for the sprint.
  2. The development itself. At the beginning of each workday, a daily scrum is held to keep team members appraised of emerging problems. See here for more information on the daily scrum.
  3. After the development time is up, the team holds a sprint review, where they demo the software that was built during the sprint to the users.
  4. The team then holds a retrospective. The team critically analyzes their performance to decide what to keep doing, what to stop doing, and some new things to try. See here for more information on retrospectives.

To ensure that the team uses the scrum methodology effectively, a scrum master is appointed. See here for more on the scrum master.
